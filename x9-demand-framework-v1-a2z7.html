Part I

The Tenant

01

Who is actually renting in similar buildings near your site right now?

Check CoStar tenant data, or local market reports. Not who you hope will rent from you — who is signing leases today.

02

What size apartment are they renting?

CoStar breaks absorption down by unit type. Leasing offices can also tell you which sizes are moving fastest. Look at what is being rented — not what is sitting empty.

03

What problem are tenants in those buildings solving on their own — that your building could solve for them?

Look for workarounds. A desk in the bedroom corner means no workspace. A gym membership nearby means no fitness facility in the building. A storage unit down the street means not enough space in the apartment. Every workaround is a tenant paying extra to fix a problem their building should have solved. That is your opportunity.

04

Is the number of renters like this growing, stable, or shrinking in your area?

Check employment growth, population data, and migration trends for your city. A shrinking renter pool today means vacant units tomorrow.

05

In one sentence — who is your tenant?

If you cannot write this in one sentence, stop here. Every decision that follows — rent, unit size, amenities — depends on this answer being clear. Do not move to Part II until it is.

End of Part I

If any of these five questions stopped you, the deal needs more work before capital is committed.

The tenant is the foundation of every rental housing decision. Your rent, your unit mix, your amenities, and your competitive position all depend on knowing exactly who is renting and why. A vague tenant means every number that follows is a guess — not evidence.
